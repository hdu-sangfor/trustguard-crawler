ENISA SBOM Adoption State of Play 2026: Survey Results and Analysis (June 2026, TLP-CLEAR, ISBN 978-92-9204-791-7, DOI 10.2824/0741767). Analyses a survey of 334 organisations (about 65% EU-based, more than 80% directly impacted by the CRA) run at the end of 2025 on how organisations are approaching SBOM adoption in response to the EU Cyber Resilience Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/2847). Central finding: the CRA acts as an accelerator for SBOM adoption. 78% have already initiated their SBOM journey (44% in pilot/limited adoption, 25% broadly adopted in products, 9% mature/fully automated); 79% expect to reach the required SBOM maturity level by the time the CRA becomes fully applicable (December 2027). Formats: 44% CycloneDX, 29% SPDX, 11% no standard format, 17% proprietary. Section 1.2 summarises CRA SBOM obligations (generate SBOM for every product with digital elements; commonly used machine-readable format; cover at least top-level dependencies; keep up to date over the lifecycle; include in technical documentation and provide to market surveillance authorities on request, no obligation to make public; integrate into the vulnerability handling process). Perceived value: risk reduction and cost avoidance (37%), operational efficiency (29%), contractual/competitive edge (26%). Main barriers: SBOM completeness (62%), data quality (37%), vulnerability matching (58% combined), lack of internal skills (28%). Top needs: an SBOM reference implementation (ranked #1), a profile defining a ‘good enough’ SBOM (31%), standardisation of SBOM formats and required fields (29%), and an SBOM-driven risk assessment framework (20%). Only 10% of consumer organisations have mandatory SBOM clauses in supplier contracts (55% in the process of adding them); 45% report that only 0-25% of their suppliers meet SBOM expectations.
Survey report; does not itself impose vulnerability disclosure timelines, CVD or PSIRT obligations, but reports on how organisations use SBOM for vulnerability management under the CRA. Section 2.4.5 (Vulnerability Management): 76% rate receiving vulnerability status / exploitability claims from suppliers as critical (39%) or important (37%), underscoring the CRA’s coordinated vulnerability disclosure approach. Preferred delivery of such claims: automatically as vulnerabilities are found, with proactive supplier notification (44%), or via a standardised API providing up-to-date vulnerability status per SBOM component (40%). Vulnerability identification and patching is the primary intended SBOM use case for 29% of organisations. Vulnerability matching (CPE/PURL alignment, false positives) is a major adoption barrier (35% ‘quite a lot’, 23% ‘extremely challenging’). Supplier-SBOM quality gaps include inadequate vulnerability references (12%). The report references CRA Article-level vulnerability-handling obligations rather than establishing new ones.
