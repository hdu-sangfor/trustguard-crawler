Secure Your Cybersecurity Foundations
Part 2. Risk Management Basics: Taming the Risk Beast
Welcome back to Simplifying Cyber! In Part 1, we laid the critical groundwork by focusing on implementing baseline cybersecurity controls – the essential foundation for any robust defence strategy. We discussed using NIST CSF 2.0 as a high-level guide and the practical CIS Controls (starting with IG1) as an implementation toolkit.
In Part 2, we will tackle what’s seen as a complex beast: Risk Management. Many organisations get stuck here, drowning in spreadsheets, complex methodologies, or simply not knowing where to begin. But just as with controls, starting simple and building practical foundations is key. We will begin by explaining the importance of framing risks aligning to NIST CSF 2.0. Next, we will explore the easy way to kickstart risk management processes by reverse engineering risks, using work from previously established baseline controls. Finally, we will look at the future of risk management powered by automation and AI, including efficiencies and integration with GRC platforms.
Getting Started: Frame Risks with NIST CSF 2.0
Remember the NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0 Functions discussed in Part 1? Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover. These aren't just great for explaining security program to executives; they also provide an excellent, easy-to-understand structure for organising risks.
Instead of starting with a blank page and trying to brainstorm every possible cyber threat, use these functions as initial high-level categories or "parent risks." Think of them as the main folders to file more specific risks later.

The Easy Way: Reverse Engineer Risks from Your Controls
Traditional risk assessment often involves identifying assets, threats, and vulnerabilities, often a lengthy process. A simplified approach flips the script: focus on the controls that should be in place (chosen baseline, like CIS IG1) and define the risk of not having them properly implemented.

This reverse engineering approach provides a direct, actionable link between control implementation efforts and risk management process. It makes building that first risk register much less daunting because it is starting with tangible control gaps rather than abstract threats. You are essentially documenting the "why" behind implementing each baseline control.
