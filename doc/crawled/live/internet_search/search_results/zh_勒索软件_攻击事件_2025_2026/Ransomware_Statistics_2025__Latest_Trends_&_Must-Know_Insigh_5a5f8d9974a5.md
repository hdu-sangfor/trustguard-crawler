Ransomware Statistics and Ransomware Trends 2025
Understand how ransomware is shaping cybersecurity priorities in 2025.
2026 THREAT LANDSCAPE REPORTThere is no good news about ransomware statistics for 2025. Attacks continue to increase as cybercriminals use data infiltrations and the threat of data leaks to pressurize companies for ransom. Even if a company can restore data from backups, leaked data from a company that refuses to pay ransom may appear on database websites operated by threat actors.
Statista reveals that in 2023, roughly 7 in 10 global cyberattacks were ransomware, with over 317 million attempts recorded. Another report by Statista states that the total amount of money received by ransomware actors was $1.1 billion in 2023, an increase of 140% from $457 million in the prior year.
That’s not all. The FBI’s 2024 IC3 Report revealed that in 2024, the IC3 received 3,156 complaints (up 11.7% from the previous year) identified as ransomware attacks with adjusted losses of over $12.4 million. This reflects the financial impact that ransomware incidents continue to inflict on businesses.
Criminal gangs are now subcontracting much of their attacks. Some offer ransomware-as-a-service (RaaS), a model popularized by early operators such as the DarkSide hacking group, allowing cybercriminals to scale attacks without developing their own tooling. Bad actors offer ransomware tools for a fee or as a portion of the illegal proceeds. This lets threat actors rent the services they need rather than having to develop their own tools or software, allowing them to increase the volume of the attacks.
RaaS thrives on affiliates and initial access brokers (IABs), who sell stolen credentials or access and enable even low-skilled actors to launch sophisticated attacks. European Union Agency for Cybersecurity (ENISA) 2024 report links this affiliate-driven model to the increase in ransomware incidents. It reflects how hacker-for-hire services lower barriers and expand attack volumes. Furthermore, the report highlights that the primary goal of cybercrime actors remains financial gain, with attacks often opportunistic and indiscriminate. Hacker-for-hire actors, sometimes tied to state-sponsored groups, fuel this professionalized ecosystem by offering RaaS services, selling access through IABs, and even conducting vulnerability exploitation for hire.
LockBit exemplifies the RaaS model. According to the UK’s National Crime Agency, the group launched more than 7,000 attacks globally between June 2022 and February 2024, before its leader Dmitry Khoroshev was unmasked and sanctioned.
The fall of LockBit did not slow the RaaS economy. Other groups quickly filled the gap. According to Cyfirma, Qilin became the most active ransomware group by June 2025, carrying out 81 attacks in a single month, a sharp 47.3% rise. Besides, ransomware groups like Akira also grew, but at a slower pace, at 9.7%, while Play saw a 31.8% drop and Safepay declined by 62.5%, highlighting a pullback. Meanwhile, Dragonforce surged dramatically, with attacks jumping 212.5%. These trends and statistics reflect how quickly the ransomware landscape is shifting.
In 2024, the ransomware landscape experienced 5,414 disclosed attacks on organizations worldwide.
Ransomware is responsible for about 20% of all cyberattacks, making it one of the most prevalent and costly threats.
Around 10 million people were affected by ransomware attacks that specifically targeted Android users.
The top five ransomware attacks in 2024 include Change Healthcare, CDK Global, Kawasaki Motors Europe, Starbucks supply chain disruption, and NHS London. Each resulted in data breaches and operational disruptions.
Around 20% of all attacks are classified as ransomware, where the hacker demands a ransom in exchange for sensitive data and assets of an organization.
Over 1.7 million ransomware attacks occur every day. This means there’s an attack every 19 seconds.
