Why Quantum Computing is Crypto’s Biggest Threat (And Why Nobody’s Panicking Yet)
Beneath the surface of the entire cryptocurrency ecosystem lies a time ticking clock. One that is ready and set to detonate at any hour.
This clock is real and is called “quantum computing”.
Here is what is up: Google achieved “quantum supremacy” in 2019 with their 53-qubit Sycamore processor that had the power to compute with speed and scale. This achievement was a landmark for computing that showed the world that quantum computing is no longer theoretical.
Today, companies like IBM and Google are already providing public access to quantum processors via cloud — and to be honest — it doesn’t look like it will be long before attacks and warning without.
THAT is a problem for cryptocurrency users because most cryptocurrencies rely on digital signatures, which quantum algorithms can potentially break, permitting attackers to forge transactions without private keys.
What makes it more worrisome is that despite quantum computing becoming one of biggest threats to crypto, nobody is really panicking. Let’s talk about it today.
How Does Crypto Actually Work?
When you carry out a transaction in the blockchain, you basically control a private key. This private key is not a simple password. Rather, it is a randomly generated number created by complex and mathematical processes which basically give you cryptographic authority over your digital funds.
As a crypto user, when you carry out a transaction like sending or receiving another wallet through a network, you are reliant on public-key cryptography. This system is specifically designed to prove ownership without revealing your identity. It is also one of the cornerstone benefits of decentralization.
So, here’s your answer to how does crypto work: everything is derived from that private key, and one-way mathematical functions are used to derive a public key. This public key is then used to generate signatures for transactions which generate your wallet address.
The thing is that this process only works in one direction.
So, it’s computationally easy to go forward: from private key to public key to address. But not the other way round. So, knowing anyone’s wallet address does not reveal their public key or private key. Even if a public key is exposed, it is almost impossible to derive anyone’s private key from it because of mathematics with astronomical complexity.
This entire structure is the very basis of cryptographic security that cryptocurrency offers.
With today’s computing power, reversing these cryptographic functions takes around billions of years, which simply makes cryptocurrency secure — not because it is unbreakable, but because it is computationally infeasible.
Enter Quantum Computers
Quantum computing is breaking it all. Currently, our blockchain technology considers itself secure because reversing the one-way mathematical functions requires impossible amount of time. However, with quantum computing crypto, you get algorithms which use quantum mechanics to solve problems and can break elliptic curve cryptography (ECC) in a matter of hours, or even seconds.
Basically, when you get a sufficiently powerful quantum computer capable for cryptographic attacks, the “one-way” process stops being one-way and becomes reversible… and when that point comes, attackers won’t need phishing or malware anymore; they could simply observe transactions and compute private keys to get full control as if they are the original owner of the wallet.
The threat isn’t quantum computers “hacking” crypto; it is them making the mathematics that crypto relies on, no longer hard enough to protect it.
So When Should We Actually Worry?
Here’s where it gets interesting: We currently don’t have quantum computers that are powerful enough to break crypto. Currently, we have achieved quantum supremacy with limited systems, and Google’s most powerful processor is 1,000 qubits. IBM estimates to hit a million-qubit system by 2033 which will become a problem because estimates suggest we need about 20 million qubits to break Bitcoin’s encryption in a reasonable timeframe.
Most cryptography experts think we are looking at 2030 to 2035 before quantum computers become a real threat to blockchain security. This is when assumptions will collapse, and security will require new standards to survive.
But, you see, we cannot sit back and do nothing meanwhile. Because, by the time quantum computers can break crypto, it will be too late to fix it. When it comes to blockchain security, you cannot just upgrade it like you update your phone. The digital assets that are secured with today’s vulnerable cryptography will still be vulnerable tomorrow.
The “Store Now, Decrypt Later” Problem
We have an even bigger problem on the horizon as well, and not enough people are talking about it. It is the “store now, decrypt later” problem. What it means is encrypted data on the blockchain, that is secure today, can be recorded and saved for later. When, in the future more powerful computers exist, this same data can be decrypted.
So, basically, there is a high chance many adversaries are recording encrypted blockchain transactions right now, just storing them, and waiting. So that when quantum computers become powerful enough, they can crack open all that stored data and exploit it with future capabilities.
Why is that a problem, you ask? Well, your crypto transaction from 2025 could be compromised a decade later, even if you have “moved” your funds. This is because historical data will still remain on the blockchain; it stays forever.
So, Why Aren’t People Freaking Out???
For one, people think 10 years is a LONG time and maybe technology will adapt, or solutions will emerge. In the crypto space, most people are worried about the next bull run or the price, or even the memes — but not a cryptographic threat that is a decade away.
Secondly, this whole situation still sounds like a concept out of a sci-fi; it is hard to visualize. So even technical people with expertise don’t really feel it despite the risk of everything.
Thirdly, we have the “assumption” mentality that someone else will fix it. I mean, anyone’s first thought would be that surely Bitcoin or Ethereum developers are working on this, right? or standards bodies will intervene? or protocols will upgrade. Right?
Maybe. Maybe not. This is not a simple software patch, you know. We are talking about the foundational cryptography of blockchains that secure value with mathematics using assumptions and time.
What Actually Needs to Happen
The cryptocurrency industry doesn’t need panic: true. But it sure needs a transition; more specifically, a transition towards post-quantum cryptography. This means moving away from classical signature schemes and reliance on elliptic curve cryptography, to quantum-resistant algorithms with reliance on lattice-based mathematics.
Under the hood, modern post-quantum signature schemes like ML-DSA (also known as Dilithium) aren’t just “lattice-based” in a vague sense. They are built on very specific mathematical problems called Module-LWE (Learning With Errors) and Module-SIS (Short Integer Solution).
In simple terms, these problems are designed so that even with enormous computing power, finding the “secret” hidden inside noisy, high-dimensional math remains infeasible. What matters is that breaking these systems is mathematically connected to solving some of the hardest known problems in lattice cryptography, such as variants of the Shortest Vector Problem (SVP).
You don’t need to understand the math to understand the takeaway: these are problems that quantum computers are not known to efficiently solve, which is why they form the backbone of today’s post-quantum security standards.

The challenge now isn’t an invention, it is adoption.
The Uncomfortable Truth
Quantum computing isn’t some theoretical concept or a distant science experiment; it is a ticking time bomb. We are facing a unique challenge in the cryptocurrency industry, which is not just innovation or regulation, but the very math that secures billions of digital assets.
The good news is we have the tools and standards for post-quantum security. Forward-looking, next-generation platforms, like ARMchain, are already building quantum-resistant systems for long-term asset protection.
But the bad news? It is adoption. Thousands of blockchain networks, millions of users, and billions of dollars in asset value all rely on cryptography that will eventually become vulnerable. However, we are simply not moving fast enough to adopt a quantum-resistant framework.
The problem is realizing this is no longer ‘optional’. Ignoring the quantum threat won’t make it go away. But you, not acting, sure will bring risk for your assets. Remember, the time to act is now; not a decade later when quantum computing is ready to strike. So, act wisely.
