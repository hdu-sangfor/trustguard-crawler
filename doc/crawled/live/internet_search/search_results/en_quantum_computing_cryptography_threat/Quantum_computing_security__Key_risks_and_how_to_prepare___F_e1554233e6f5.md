How Quantum Computing Security Protects Encryption And Data
Learn how businesses can prepare for quantum threats and protect sensitive data.
WEBINAR: QUANTUM ERA PREPARATION 2026 THREAT LANDSCAPE REPORTKey Takeaways
Quantum computing security refers to the strategies and cryptographic methods used to protect data against the threats posed by quantum computers. These machines solve complex mathematical problems at unprecedented speeds. This renders traditional encryption methods like RSA and ECC obsolete, threatening sensitive data, critical infrastructure, and secure communications.
FBI, NIST, and CISA have designated 2026 the Year of Quantum Security, reflecting the urgency of transitioning to quantum-safe encryption. Quantum computers can break the mathematical foundations that underpin public-key encryption, making proactive action essential for every organization.
The quantum threat is no longer theoretical. It is here now. In March 2026, Google Quantum AI research found that a quantum computer with fewer than one million qubits could break RSA-2048 encryption in under a week. According to The Quantum Insider, this is a tenfold reduction from prior estimates. NIST has finalized its post-quantum cryptographic standards. The NSA mandates that all new National Security Systems be quantum-safe by January 2027. According to the WEF Global Cybersecurity Outlook 2026, 37% of respondents expect quantum technologies to affect cybersecurity within 12 months. Organizations must build crypto agility into their security strategy now.
Google’s March 2026 research demonstrated that a quantum computer with fewer than 1 million noisy physical qubits could factor RSA-2048 in under a week — a tenfold reduction from prior estimates of 20 million qubits.
Unlike classical computers that use bits to process information (0 or 1), quantum computers use quantum bits or qubits. These bits exist simultaneously in multiple states (superposition) and can be entangled with each other. This allows quantum computers to perform complex calculations effortlessly.
They will be able to break traditional encryption, causing potential data breaches and business disruptions. To understand when this shift is expected to happen, learn about what is Q-Day.
The unique capabilities of quantum computers present four threats to cybersecurity.
Cryptographic vulnerability: Quantum computing has the potential to break cryptographic schemes like RSA and DSA, which secure data. Algorithms like Shor's algorithm can work on large number factorization faster than classical algorithms, making the public-key infrastructure (PKI) highly vulnerable. This is a significant risk as the PKI underpins authentication mechanisms, secure communications, and more.
Harvest now, decrypt later: This threat vector involves accumulating encrypted data with the expectation that future quantum computing advancements will render current cryptographic defenses obsolete. It is imperative for organizations to adopt quantum-safe algorithms today in order to protect themselves tomorrow.
Exponential speed attack: Using algorithms like Grover's algorithm, quantum computers can perform search and pattern recognition faster than classical counterparts. This significantly reduces the time required for brute force attacks on symmetric encryption.
Advanced quantum malware: Advanced quantum capabilities combined with AI will enable threat actors to develop sophisticated forms of malware that can evade current detection mechanisms, leading to multivariate and multi-front attacks. Quantum-enabled ML algorithms can automate the discovery of zero-day vulnerabilities across endpoints, allowing quicker and more sophisticated attacks.
